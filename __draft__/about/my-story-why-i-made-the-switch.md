## The Question I Get Asked

*"So... you went from managing electrical systems to data engineering. What happened?"*

People ask like I made a drastic career change. Like I woke up one day and decided electrical systems weren't cool anymore. 

That's not really how it happened.

---

## The Real Story

I didn't leave electrical engineering. **I expanded it.**

For 14 years, I did the job right. Managed electrical infrastructure at a water utility. Fixed systems at 2 AM when things broke. Optimized power consumption. Hired and led teams. Dealt with real consequences when decisions went wrong.

But somewhere around year 12-13, I noticed a pattern.

We were doing the same calculations. Every month. Manually. Looking at sensor data. Trying to spot patterns. Asking questions like:
- *"Why does load spike at 5 PM?"*
- *"Can we predict when this pump will fail?"*
- *"Are we optimizing for the wrong metrics?"*

And the answer every time was: *"We don't have good tools for this."*

---

## The Aha Moment

One day, I was sitting in the control room, staring at spreadsheets of sensor data, and it hit me:

> **"Someone has already solved this problem. And it's not called 'electrical engineering.' It's called 'data engineering.'"**

Not as a replacement for what I do. As a **superpower for what I already do.**

I wasn't abandoning electrical systems. I was discovering I could understand them WAY better with the right tools and techniques.

---

## Why Data Engineering Called To Me

### Problem 1: The Patterns
In electrical systems, **data tells stories** if you know how to listen. Load profiles. Efficiency curves. Anomalies. Trends. All hiding in numbers.

I realized: *"I've been staring at data for 14 years. Why not actually learn to extract insights from it?"*

### Problem 2: The Efficiency
We were doing things the hard way. Manual reports. Spreadsheet hell. No automation. No pipelines. Just humans re-doing the same work monthly.

I thought: *"What if I could build systems that did this automatically?"*

### Problem 3: The Prediction
Equipment fails unexpectedly. People respond to problems after they happen. But what if we could see failures coming?

*"There has to be a way to predict this with historical data..."*

---

## The Decision

I didn't quit electrical engineering. I started learning data engineering **on the side**.

Python. SQL. ETL pipelines. Data modeling. Machine learning. Statistics.

Not to escape what I was doing. To **level up** what I was doing.

And here's the weird part: it worked.

Suddenly, I could:
- ✅ Spot efficiency problems nobody else saw
- ✅ Predict equipment failures before they happened
- ✅ Automate reports that took hours
- ✅ Answer questions people weren't even asking yet
- ✅ See the electrical system through a completely different lens

**I became a better electrical engineer by learning data engineering.**

---

## The Fork in the Road

After 3 years of freelancing on data projects while still working at the utility, I faced a choice:

**Option A:** Keep doing both. Stay comfortable.

**Option B:** Go all-in on data engineering. Take the leap.

Here's why I chose B:

The data problems became MORE interesting to me than maintaining existing systems.

Not because electrical systems stopped being cool. They didn't.

But because **solving the problem** became more interesting than **managing the problem**.

---

## What I Actually Did

I didn't leave electrical engineering. I **changed my focus** within it.

Instead of "How do I keep this system running?" I started asking:
- "How do I make this system predictive?"
- "How do I understand this system better?"
- "How do I build tools that others can use?"

This is **still electrical engineering thinking**. Just applied differently.

---

## The Weird Part

Here's what confuses people:

I have a Bachelor's in Electrical Engineering. 14 years experience in electrical systems. Real expertise in power systems, industrial automation, PLC programming.

And now I'm a "Data Engineer."

People see this as a **pivot**. A career change. An escape from engineering.

But I don't see it that way.

I see it as: **I'm an electrical engineer who discovered the most powerful tool for solving electrical problems was data.**
